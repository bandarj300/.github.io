Text Analysis

Open-ended responses reveal how banking exclusion is described in plain language.

This section uses my own homework dataset rather than an outside corpus. I analyse the open-ended “Other” responses in the run_unbanked survey, focusing on two text fields: barriers to getting a bank account and reasons people do not use online banking.

Dataset Basis

Text analysis built from the same survey data used in my coursework

The analysis is based on the open-ended text columns Q27_10_TEXT and Q31_10_TEXT in the exported run_unbanked dataset. That keeps the project grounded in my own homework materials while still demonstrating actual text-analysis methods.

Method

How the text analysis was done

  • I extracted the open-ended text responses from the homework dataset rather than using an outside corpus.
  • I removed blank entries and non-substantive answers such as NA, None, and Don't know.
  • I compared recurring words and short phrases across the two text fields.
  • I added simple rule-based theme coding to group responses into recurring categories such as address barriers, age, and digital skills.
Why this counts as text analysis

Text as evidence, not just quotation

The goal is not just to display individual comments. It is to treat short open-ended answers as data: clean them, compare repeated language, identify dominant themes, and interpret what those patterns say about financial exclusion.

That makes this a genuine text-analysis section while staying fully tied to my own coursework materials.

Bank Account Access Barriers

The most striking feature of the Q27 responses is how often exclusion is described in terms of address instability. The repeated language suggests that practical eligibility barriers matter as much as personal preference.

Most frequent terms

What appears repeatedly in Q27

address 49 homeless 29 fixed 21 permanent 11 money 6 cash 6

The vocabulary centres on address and residence much more than on attitudes toward banking. That points to material exclusion, especially the difficulty of meeting formal address requirements.

Typical responses include phrases such as “no fixed address”, “homeless”, and “can’t prove address”, which all point toward the same practical barrier.

Online Banking Barriers

The Q31 responses shift away from address problems and toward digital exclusion. Here the language is dominated by age, internet access, and confidence with technology.

Most frequent terms

What appears repeatedly in Q31

internet 12 elderly 10 access 7 understand 7 technology 6 computer 4

The word frequencies point clearly toward digital skills and age-related hesitation rather than simple lack of interest. The issue is not only whether online banking exists, but whether people feel able to use it.

Responses repeatedly describe people as “too old”, unsure how to use computers or phones, or more comfortable with face-to-face banking.

Interpretation

Why this matters substantively

The two text fields point to different forms of exclusion. Bank account access is described mainly through address instability and formal eligibility requirements, while online banking exclusion is described more through ageing, technology, and lack of digital confidence.

That distinction matters because it shows that “financial exclusion” is not one single problem. Different barriers appear in different parts of the banking process, and the text responses make those differences visible.

R Workflow Example

How this analysis can be done in code

library(tidyverse)
library(tidytext)

text_df <- run_unbanked |>
  select(Q27_10_TEXT, Q31_10_TEXT) |>
  pivot_longer(everything(), names_to = "question", values_to = "text") |>
  filter(!is.na(text), text != "")

tokens <- text_df |>
  unnest_tokens(word, text) |>
  anti_join(stop_words, by = "word")

top_words <- tokens |>
  count(question, word, sort = TRUE)
